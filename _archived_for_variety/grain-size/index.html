Grain Size Analysis for Construction Projects in Birmingham AL

We see it all the time. A contractor in Birmingham starts a foundation dig near the Red Mountain foothills, runs into unexpected silt, and suddenly the project is delayed by two weeks because no one ran a grain size analysis beforehand. The soil here isn't uniform. The city sits at the intersection of the Valley and Ridge and the Coastal Plain, so you get coarse residual soils from weathered shale and sandstone mixed with fine alluvial deposits in the lower elevations. Skipping a particle size distribution test means you're guessing on drainage, compaction, and even frost susceptibility. For any job near the Jones Valley or along the Cahaba River floodplain, we combine Proctor tests to set compaction specs once the gradation is known.

If your soil classifies as SM when you assumed SW, your bearing capacity just dropped by half. Gradation isn't paperwork. It's load path.

Methodology and scope

ASTM D422 and D6913 drive everything we do in the lab. Birmingham's geological setting makes the hydrometer portion of ASTM D422 especially important. Many of the soils in the metro area, particularly those derived from the Conasauga Formation, contain a significant amount of fine-grained material that passes through a No. 200 sieve. If you're working in a commercial development out toward Hoover, the soil might look like a clean sand but the silt content can push it into a different classification entirely under the Unified Soil Classification System. That shift changes your allowable bearing pressure and your approach to drainage. We run the full stack: a nest of sieves for the coarse fraction and a 152H hydrometer for the fine fraction. When the gradation curve shows a gap, we flag it immediately. For deeper projects, a CPT test can validate the profile without disturbing the sample.

Local considerations

Soil in the Avondale area behaves completely differently than soil out toward Bessemer. Avondale sits on residual clays and silts from the Pottsville Formation: lots of fine particles, moderate plasticity, lousy drainage. Bessemer, on the other hand, has patches of alluvial sand that drain fast but can be loose and prone to settlement. Without a grain size analysis, you're treating both sites the same way. That's a recipe for callbacks. The biggest headache we see is when a contractor designs a stormwater infiltration basin using a textbook infiltration rate, but the actual gradation curve from our lab shows a D10 of 0.03 mm. That basin won't drain; it'll become a pond. Our lab gives you the numbers so you can size your systems correctly.

Associated technical services

01

Full Sieve Analysis

Mechanical shaking through a stack of sieves from 3 inches down to No. 200. We report percent retained and passing by weight. Ideal for sands and gravels common in Shelby County road projects.

02

Hydrometer Analysis

Sedimentation test for particles smaller than 0.075 mm. We use a 152H hydrometer and record readings at 2, 5, 15, 30, 60, 250, and 1440 minutes. Necessary for the silty clays in the downtown Birmingham area.

03

Combined Gradation Curve

We merge the coarse and fine fractions into a single semi-log plot. Includes D10, D30, D60, and calculation of Cu and Cc. Delivered as a signed PDF with our lab stamp within two business days.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a grain size analysis cost for a Birmingham project?

A standard combined sieve and hydrometer analysis runs between US$100 and US$180 per sample, depending on whether we need to wash the fines or run a full hydrometer series. Volume discounts apply for 10 or more samples from the same site.

Do I need the hydrometer portion for a sandy soil?

If more than 5% of the material passes the No. 200 sieve, ASTM D422 requires the hydrometer for an accurate classification. A lot of Birmingham soils look sandy in the field but carry enough silt to cross the threshold. We'll run a wash sieve first to check, then decide with you.

What's the minimum sample size you need?

For combined analysis, we need a minimum of 500 grams of material passing the No. 4 sieve, plus the coarse fraction retained. Our team can walk you through the sampling procedure so you don't show up with a bag of gravel and no fines.
